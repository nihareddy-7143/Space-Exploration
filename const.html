Northern Hemisphere Constellations:


Ursa Major (The Great Bear):

Recognized by the distinctive shape known as the "Big Dipper" or the "Plough." Contains the famous asterism called the "Big Dipper," which is often used for navigation.


Orion: One of the most recognizable constellations with the prominent "Orion's Belt." Contains the bright stars Betelgeuse and Rigel.

Cassiopeia: Named after the queen in Greek mythology and has a distinctive "W" or "M" shape. Circumpolar in northern latitudes, meaning it is visible year-round.

Draco (The Dragon): A large constellation winding between Ursa Major and Ursa Minor. Contains the star Thuban, which was once the North Star around 4,000 years ago.

Southern Hemisphere Constellations:


Crux (The Southern Cross): One of the smallest constellations but highly recognizable. Features prominently on the flags of several Southern Hemisphere countries.
Centaurus: Home to Alpha Centauri, the closest star system to Earth. Contains the prominent star Hadar.
Southern Triangle: Not a single constellation but an asterism formed by three bright stars: Acrux, Beta Centauri, and Alpha Circini. Pegasus: Named after the mythical winged horse. Contains the prominent square of stars known as the "Great Square of Pegasus."
Carina (The Keel): Formerly part of the larger constellation Argo Navis. Home to the impressive Eta Carinae Nebula.

What constellations can you see in the night sky? The constellations you can see at night depend on the time of year. Earth orbits around the Sun once each year. Our view into space through the night sky changes as we orbit. So, the night sky looks slightly different each night because Earth is in a different spot in its orbit. The stars appear each night to move slightly west of where they were the night before.
